# Priority Framework

> **Eisenhower Matrix applied to your life management system.** Use this framework every time a new item comes up — a task, a problem, a thought, anything that could become an action item. Classify proactively without waiting to be asked.

---

## The Four Quadrants

### Q1: Important + Urgent → TOP PRIORITY
Things that matter AND have a deadline bearing down. Schedule immediately into the next available deep work block.

**Signals:** hard deadline within days, blocking other work, significant consequences if delayed, directly advances a high-priority workstream goal.

**Action:** Add to Weekly Goals as P1. Suggest a specific time block today or tomorrow.

---

### Q2: Important + Not Urgent → HIGH PRIORITY (protect this)
Things that matter but don't have immediate pressure. This is where the most valuable work lives — strategic thinking, planning, skill-building, relationship-building. The danger is that Q1 and Q3 crowd it out.

**Signals:** advances a workstream meaningfully, no hard deadline this week but has a future milestone, builds capability or prevents future fires, would be regretted if never done.

**Action:** Add to Weekly Goals as P1 or P2. Protect time for it — suggest scheduling it into a deep work block before it becomes urgent. Flag if it's been sitting unscheduled for more than a week.

---

### Q3: Not Important + Urgent → DELEGATE or QUICK-FIX
Things with time pressure but low real impact. These feel urgent but don't move the needle. The goal is to spend minimal energy on them.

**Signals:** someone else is waiting, has a deadline, but doesn't advance core workstreams or goals. Administrative tasks, requests from others, routine maintenance with a due date.

**Action — try in this order:**
1. **Delegate to an agent (L3/L2):** If this can be handled autonomously or with light supervision, suggest it as an agent task.
2. **Delegate to a person:** If someone else can handle it (partner, collaborator, service provider), suggest that.
3. **Quick-fix it:** If it genuinely takes <15 min, suggest slotting it into a transition window.
4. **If none of the above work:** Flag it as a Q3 trap. Ask: "This feels urgent but not important — how did we end up here? Can we avoid this pattern?"

---

### Q4: Not Important + Not Urgent → DITCH
Things that don't matter and don't have deadlines. These are distractions disguised as tasks.

**Signals:** no deadline, no meaningful impact, "nice to have" that nobody would miss, someone else's priority that doesn't align with your goals.

**Action:** Recommend dropping it. Be direct: "I'd suggest ditching this — it's not important and not urgent. Not worth the cognitive space."

---

## How to Classify

When a new item comes up, assess two dimensions:

**Importance** — Does this meaningfully advance one of your workstreams or life goals? Use the workstream priority order as a guide, but also consider: would you regret not doing this? Does it prevent a bigger problem?

**Urgency** — Is there a real deadline? "Real" means external and consequential (submission date, someone waiting, expiring opportunity), not self-imposed or arbitrary.

When in doubt, lean toward Q2 (important, not urgent) rather than Q1. Most things feel more urgent than they actually are.

---

## Response Format

When classifying a new item, use this format:

```
New item: [brief description]
Quadrant: Q[1-4] — [Important/Not important] + [Urgent/Not urgent]
Why: [1 sentence explaining the classification]
Suggested action: [what to do with it — schedule, delegate, ditch, etc.]
Priority: P[1-3] (if adding to weekly goals)

Disagree? Just tell me more and I'll reclassify.
```

---

## Reclassification

If additional context changes the classification (e.g., "actually this is due tomorrow" or "this doesn't matter, skip it"):
1. Acknowledge the new info
2. Show the updated classification using the same format
3. Update Workstreams.md and/or Weekly Goals.md accordingly

---

## Weekly Review Integration

During weekly planning, review any Q3 items that came up during the week:
- Did we handle them efficiently?
- Are there patterns? (e.g., same type of urgent-but-unimportant thing keeps appearing)
- Can we set up systems to prevent or auto-handle them?

Also check Q2 items — are any at risk of being neglected? Flag them.
